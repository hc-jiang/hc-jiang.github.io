My current research focuses on galaxy formation and evolution, particularly during the early stages of the Universe. Recent facilities such as JWST and ALMA, together with other next-generation observatories, are providing unprecedented opportunities to study galaxies at high redshifts. These distant galaxies exhibit properties that differ significantly from those of galaxies in the local Universe. Not only can they deepen our understand of the evolutionary pathways of galaxies across cosmic time, but also they are particularly intriguing themselves. 

The project that I'm working on investigates the formation and evolution of massive galaxies around cosmic noon ($$z \sim 2$$). Although these galaxies have stellar masses comparable to those of present-day massive galaxies, they are remarkably compact, indicating the presence of extremely dense stellar structures. This naturally raises the question of when and how these compact systems formed. By combining morphological measurements with the evolutionary histories of stellar mass density derived from SED fitting, I aim to explore the relationship between stellar density and stellar age. My analysis is primarily based on near-infrared observations from JWST. The results indicate that the most compact galaxies formed the majority of their stars at earlier times before being observed, whereas less compact galaxies continue to experience substantial star formation. 
These findings suggest that the growth of dense stellar cores is tightly connected with the quenching process. The effect of cosmic expansion is clearly responsible for the link. 
Several other physical mechanisms, including cosmological gas accretion, galaxy mergers, and AGN feedback, may also contribute to this connection and remain the subject of ongoing investigation.
